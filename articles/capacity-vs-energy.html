Battery Capacity vs Energy: They're Not the Same Thing

Battery capacity and energy are often confused, but they represent different aspects of a battery's performance.

Reading Time: 2 minutes 30 seconds

Author: Srijal Dutta

Date: 29-06-2026

Capacity and Energy Are Different

Battery capacity and battery energy are closely related, but they describe different characteristics of a battery. As discussed in our previous article on battery capacity, capacity tells us how much electrical charge a battery can store. Energy, however, describes how much total work that stored charge can perform. Understanding this distinction is essential when comparing different battery technologies.

What Is Battery Energy?

Battery energy is usually measured in watt-hours (Wh). While capacity tells us how much charge is available, energy also takes voltage into account. A battery with a higher voltage can deliver more energy than another battery with the same capacity because each unit of charge carries more electrical potential. This is why two batteries with identical ampere-hour ratings can store different amounts of usable energy.

How Voltage and Capacity Work Together

Earlier, we explored what makes a battery produce voltage and what limits battery current. Energy combines these concepts with capacity. A battery's total energy depends on both the electrical potential it provides and the amount of charge it stores. Increasing either voltage or capacity increases the total energy available.

Why It Matters

Looking only at capacity can sometimes be misleading. For example, two batteries may both be rated at 2 Ah, but if one operates at 12 volts and the other at 24 volts, the second battery stores twice as much energy. Engineers therefore often compare batteries using watt-hours when evaluating how long they can power a device or how much work they can perform.

A Simple Analogy

Imagine two identical water tanks. Both hold the same amount of water, representing equal battery capacity. However, one tank is placed on a much taller tower. Since the water falls from a greater height, it can perform more work, such as turning a water wheel more effectively. The amount of water represents capacity, while the height represents voltage. Together, they determine the total energy available.

Conclusion

Capacity tells us how much electrical charge a battery can store, while energy tells us how much total work that charge can perform. Both measurements are important, but they answer different questions. By understanding the relationship between capacity, voltage, current, and energy, we build a complete picture of how batteries are compared and selected for different applications.
